# Forget Your Good Deeds. Forget Others' Wrongs.

### Plato's Four Principles for a Peaceful Life

> **Channel:** Atma Theory\
> **Category:** Spiritual Philosophy / Ancient Wisdom / Plato\
> **Format:** Long-form Documentary\
> **Narration Style:** Calm • Reflective • Contemplative\
> **Target Duration:** 8--10 minutes

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Opening Hook

What if the greatest burden you carry... isn't in your hands... but in
your memory?

Some memories quietly become chains.

We remember every favor we've done.

Every insult we've received.

Every disappointment.

Every betrayal.

And without realizing it...

our mind becomes heavier than our life.

More than two thousand years ago, Plato offered four remarkably simple
principles.

Two things to forget.

Two things to remember.

He believed that if a person truly lived by these four principles...

not only would that person's life become beautiful...

society itself would become more beautiful.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Question That Began Everything

One day, Plato's disciples asked,

"Master... what should we forget... and what should we always remember?"

Plato did not begin with complicated philosophy.

He first awakened their attention.

A joyful mind remains curious.

It continues asking...

"What is this?"

"What can life teach me?"

Only after drawing them into that spirit of inquiry...

did he reveal the four principles of a peaceful life.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The First Thing to Forget

Plato said,

**"Whatever good you do for others... forget it."**

Help someone.

Experience the joy of helping.

Then let it go.

The moment we begin keeping accounts...

expectation quietly enters.

Expectation becomes disappointment.

Disappointment becomes suffering.

Soon we begin saying,

"I sacrificed so much."

"I was always there."

"But did anyone remember?"

The pain was never in the helping.

It was in wanting the help to be remembered.

Plato illustrated this with a simple image.

Imagine walking along a road.

A banana peel lies in the middle of the path.

Someone could slip.

So you pick it up...

and throw it aside.

Would you remain standing there...

waiting for strangers to applaud?

Of course not.

Then why do we expect recognition for every act of kindness?

If one small good deed becomes a lifelong expectation...

its true beauty has already been lost.

Do good...

because goodness itself brings joy.

Not because someone else may remember it.

Nature never keeps accounts.

Birds fill the morning with song.

They never wait for applause.

Saints spent their lives serving humanity.

They never counted their kindness.

They served...

and moved on.

That is why Plato said,

**Forget the good you have done.**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Second Thing to Forget

Then Plato said,

**Forget the wrong others have done to you.**

What has passed...

has already passed.

Yet many of us carry an invisible diary.

"This person insulted me."

"That person betrayed me."

"They never respected me."

Page after page...

the diary fills with old wounds.

But whose life becomes miserable?

Theirs...

or ours?

Then Plato asked a question that still echoes today.

**Should your head become a dustbin?**

A dustbin is made to collect garbage.

No one fills it with jewels.

Then why should the mind become a place where insults, jealousy,
resentment and gossip are stored?

The moment we collect the world's emotional garbage...

our own peace disappears.

In old villages, elders would often smile and ask,

"Why are you bringing the village's garbage into your own home?"

Someone had returned carrying everyone's gossip.

The elder would simply say,

"Eat peacefully.

Sleep peacefully.

Why carry all that into your heart?"

How quietly profound that wisdom is.

We clean our homes before festivals.

Yet many of us never clean the mind.

Leave yesterday's arguments where they belong.

Walk into your home with a lighter heart.

That is how peace enters a family.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The First Thing to Remember

After hearing the two things to forget...

the disciples asked,

"What should we always remember?"

Plato replied,

**Never believe you are the greatest person in this world.**

Never imagine that everything revolves around you.

Behind every success...

there is a wisdom greater than our own.

A larger order.

A greater intelligence.

We are only a small part of something infinitely vast.

The moment ego grows...

peace quietly leaves.

Humility is not thinking less of yourself.

It is remembering that life is always greater than you.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Second Thing to Remember

Finally, Plato said,

**Never forget that no person... and no possession... remains with you
forever.**

Not wealth.

Not status.

Not relationships.

Everything changes.

Life is like a train journey.

Today you occupy a seat.

Tomorrow, when your station arrives...

someone else sits in the very same place.

Was it ever truly yours?

Look at your pocket.

It begins empty.

You slowly fill it.

Then, little by little...

everything leaves again.

Life itself ends...

with empty hands.

Even our children...

who once refused to leave our side...

grow up.

They build lives of their own.

Yesterday they held our hand.

Tomorrow they may live in another city...

or another country.

Love deeply.

Care completely.

But never mistake temporary companionship...

for permanence.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Final Reflection

These are Plato's four principles.

Forget the good you have done.

Forget the wrong others have done to you.

Remember that you are never the center of existence.

Remember that nothing remains forever.

When we stop counting our kindness...

we become lighter.

When we stop collecting old hurts...

we become freer.

When ego softens...

gratitude appears.

When impermanence is accepted...

every moment becomes more precious.

Perhaps peace has never been about gaining more.

Perhaps it has always been about knowing...

what to release...

and what to remember.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Closing

**This is Atma Theory.**

If these words stayed with you...

don't simply admire them.

Live them.

Let kindness leave your hands without asking to be remembered.

Let yesterday's wounds fade without asking to be avenged.

Walk with humility.

Accept impermanence.

And quietly...

allow peace to find its place within you.

**Think deeper... Live clearer.**
