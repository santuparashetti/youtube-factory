# Transcript: The Story of Bhupadeva and the Power of Effort

There was once an ashram. It was a very large ashram. In those days, ashrams were centers of education, places where gurus resided and hundreds of disciples (shishyas) lived and learned together. A calm, serene place like that is what we call an ashram. Bharat (India) became renowned across the world as a land of ashrams. 

Rishis (sages) and ashrams are completely inseparable; one cannot be separated from the other. Their connection was profoundly deep. The history of Indian storytelling begins with such rishis. Wherever sages lived, that place came to be known as an ashram. 

Who are these rishis? They are ascetics (*tapasvis*). They could be seekers of knowledge (*jnana tapasvis*), physical ascetics, or those mastering worldly sciences. Anyone dedicated to disciplined penance is a rishi, and their dwelling is an ashram.

In one such ashram, there were many disciples. Usually, they would arrive around the age of eight and spend ten to twelve years acquiring various branches of knowledge. Upon completion, the guru would grant them permission to return home to their respective towns and families. 

Among them was a particular disciple. He had arrived many years ago. All the students who entered the ashram with him, or even after him, completed their education and left, but he remained behind. He struggled to comprehend anything. whenever he asked, the Guru would tell him, "You are not yet ready; you need to stay longer." In those days, a unique feature of the ashram system was that gurus never sent a student back to society without proper knowledge and capability. They believed that to live in the world, one needs true competence. 

If a student was sent back well-educated and capable, families were eager to marry their daughters to him. If someone lacked competence, no one would offer their daughter. That was the social standard of the time. The ashram prepared individuals so thoroughly that they transformed the society they returned to, making it virtuous and righteous.

But this boy grew deeply discouraged. Every time he asked, he was told, "You aren't capable yet; do not leave." He thought to himself, "How many times should I keep asking? I simply cannot grasp anything. It doesn't enter my mind or settle in my heart. There is no use staying here. It is better to leave without telling the guru." One day, he made a firm decision. 

He slipped away quietly under the cover of night. By morning, he had walked a great distance. As the afternoon sun grew fierce, he became exhausted and intensely thirsty. He looked around but couldn't find water anywhere. He kept walking, driven by extreme thirst.

Eventually, he spotted a well nearby. It was a stone well equipped with a pulley system. Seeing the well filled him with immense joy. The water inside looked clean and refreshing. A woman was drawing water there using an earthen pot. 

He watched as she drew a pot of water and placed it down on a large stone slab next to the well. She would draw another pot, place it down, and repeat the process. The boy approached her and asked, "Could you please give me some water to drink?"

She kindly held out her hands and poured water for him to drink. As he drank, his eyes fell upon the stone slab where she kept placing the pot. He noticed a distinct, smooth depression hollowed out in the hard stone.

Stopping mid-drink, he asked out of curiosity, "Why is there a deep dent in the stone here?"

The woman replied, "It is just an earthen pot, but because it is placed down in the exact same spot time after time, day after day, its constant friction has worn away the hard stone and created this dent."

Hearing this, a sudden realization flashed through the boy's mind. He thought, "If a soft earthen pot, through continuous and repeated placement, can carve a dent into solid stone, then why can't my mind grasp knowledge if I study repeatedly with absolute persistence?" 

He realized that what he considered "impossible" was merely a lack of persistent effort. "I must discard this thought that I cannot learn," he resolved. This boy’s name was **Bhupadeva** (also known as Bopadeva), who later went on to become an extraordinarily great scholar. The simple sight of an earthen pot wearing down a stone transformed his entire perspective.

Later, even a simple rope made of woven fibers, by constantly rubbing against the hard stone edge of the well as it is pulled up and down, leaves deep grooves in the stone. Seeing this, he understood the truth. He decided not to run away and returned straight to the ashram.

When he returned, the Guru guided him, explaining: "The very first thing you must do is remove the thought of 'impossibility' from your mind. That is where true *tapas* (effort) begins."

Nothing is entirely impossible. If it doesn't happen in a year, it might take two years, or even ten years. Look at how humans have transformed barren, sandy lands into lush, wonderful landscapes. They achieved it through sheer, relentless work. It didn't happen by itself; it required intense, continuous effort.

Look at the wilderness around us. Do you think a single tree or blade of grass grew on these hard rocks without effort? Initially, it was just barren, solid rock that couldn't hold water. Yet, through persistent nurturing, beautiful greenery emerged. Why? Because someone believed it was possible and put in the work. Trees have an innate strength; if you give them even a tiny crack, their roots will eventually shatter the hardest rock to find sustenance. 

A tiny sapling possesses such immense capability that it can break through stone. When nature itself shows such resilience, why should a human being lose heart and declare, "I cannot do it"? This is the essence of *tapas*. The very first step of effort is to eliminate the word "impossible" from your mind. 

At the same time, one shouldn't fall into the delusion that everything will happen instantly without labor. You cannot simply wish to jump to the sky and expect to fly. Success comes when you combine your aspirations with structured, continuous practice. That is the deeper meaning of effort. Nothing is impossible, but if you do not practice and put in the work, you will achieve nothing.

The second step is the commencement of practice (*sadhana*). What does that entail? First and foremost, you must prepare your mind to endure hardships. No worthwhile achievement is ever born without facing difficulties. Hardships are inevitable. But a mind that says, "No matter how many difficulties come, I will endure them, face them, and overcome them," possesses *dradhata* (fortitude and determination). A seeker must have absolute determination.

As Akka Mahadevi beautifully expressed in her poetry: "Let the earth crack, let the mountains come crashing down upon me, let the entire town burn around me—I will never lose my resolve."

We must make our minds strong. At worst, what could happen? We might lose our lives, that's all. Nobody is here permanently anyway. Look at a person who is severely addicted to something harmful; even when the doctor warns him plainly, "If you continue this, you will die," the addict stubbornly replies, "Will you live forever, doctor? Everyone has to go someday, some sooner, some later." If a person can show such obstinate determination for a negative habit, shouldn't a person striving for a noble goal possess infinitely greater resolve? This stubborn, unyielding dedication to a path is the true meaning of *tapas*.

A true practitioner does not let external discouragements sway them. They hold onto an unshakeable determination. 

It is said in the scriptures:
*Udyamena hi siddhyanti kāryāṇi na manorathaiḥ |*
*Na hi suptasya siṁhasya mukhe praviśanti mṛgāḥ ||*

"Tasks are accomplished through active effort (*udyama*), not by mere wishing (*manoratha*). A deer does not walk into the mouth of a sleeping lion on its own."

No matter how powerful the lion is, or even if it is the king of the jungle, if it decides to just sleep and expects prey to walk into its mouth, it will starve. Even the king of the forest must hunt and fight for its food. Nothing comes as a free gift.

Take a tiny rabbit. It might not know how to attack a lion, but it knows exactly how to escape and survive. The rabbit thinks, "I may not be able to fight you, but I will not let you catch me easily." If the prey didn't put effort into escaping, its species would have vanished long ago. Every creature struggles and puts in effort to survive.

If a sleeping lion gets nothing without effort, what about humans? Suppose a person is lying down beneath a large mango tree. He is thirsty and hungry, and right above him hang ripe, delicious mangoes. The tree virtually whispers, "Climb up, and you shall have a sweet fruit." But if the man remains lazy, lying on the ground, expecting the fruit to drop precisely into his mouth, it won't happen. 

Waiting idly for things to happen is fatalism; actively climbing the tree to harvest the fruit is human effort (*paurusha*). 

*Udyamena hi siddhyanti kāryāṇi na manorathaiḥ...*
Success requires active striving. What is the use of merely chanting mantras or wishing internally if there is no action? Can a boy become strong and muscular just by wishing? If a student wants to become an exceptional runner and secure the first rank, can he achieve it without running? If he never runs a single lap but spends all his time reading books on "The Art of Running," memorizing entire libraries of theories without ever stepping onto the track, will he ever learn to run?

Whichever skill or goal you wish to master, you must practice that specific activity thoroughly. Only then will you achieve mastery. Doing something unrelated will yield nothing. 

People often complain, "I just cannot remember what I study. What medicine or pill should I take to improve my memory?" But the truth is, they don't study repeatedly! They read something once and expect to remember it forever. Does anyone remember a name after hearing it just once? Even when parents name a child, they repeat that name thousands of times over a year until the child begins to respond to it. If remembering a simple name requires so much repetition, how can a student expect to recall complex subjects in an exam after reading them just once?

Mastery demands continuous repetition, review, and deep reflection (*punashcharana*). You must put in the work, time and time again. That is the ultimate secret of success.
